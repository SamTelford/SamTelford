Is coffee a soup?
Matt asked me just that question a few weeks ago. Matt is a fellow student, housemate, and above all one of my closest friends. While studying on campus he had come up with the question and posed in a group chat that we share with a handful of other friends.
We ended up discussing the question at length, and in that discourse a number of valid arguments, non-sequiturs, fallacies , definitions and counterexamples were used. I’d like to go through a handful of sections of our chat and break down what made them function or fail.
Before starting, here are a few points that must be made:
  • I am a member of this conversation so there will be an unavoidable bias in my breakdown of the arguments.
  • I tried to be fair in including all the messages related to each point, but in the event that you’d like the full context of a point I’ve attached a complete copy of the entire discussion at the end of this post.
  • There are a number of possible definitions for soup, and a significant part of our conversation involves proposing a definition and seeing how well it separates examples of soups from examples that are not soups.
This first point debates the validity of the requirement for soup to have a meat or vegetable component:
1
Sam
wikipedia says soup has to have meat or vegetables
^but coffee is a fruit
Here I make an Appeal to Questionable Authority. Wikipedia is by no means a qualified source for the definitions of terms like soup. Despite this, I have proposed a definition to separate soups from non-soups. As a side note coffee beans are not beans at all, but instead the seed of the coffee fruit.
Here is the logical form of my claim:
  1. If a food does not contain meat or vegetables, it is not a soup
  2. Coffee beans come from a fruit; coffee does not contain meat or vegetables
  3. Therefore coffee is not a soup
2
Matt
Tomato soup ?
Counterexample:
Tomato soup is soup, but violates the definition of soup in 1, therefore the definition must not be correct.
It could be argued that this is an example of equivocation, and just because “tomato soup” has soup in its name it doesn’t follow that it is necessarily a soup, but as I’ll cover later on ‘soup’ is a fairly arbitrary term in the first place, so the fact that tomato soup is fairly universally considered a soup makes equivocation a poor argument at this point.
3
Sam
tomato is a culinary vegetable
In response to Matt’s counterexample I point out that in the context of culinary arts, a tomato is considered a vegetable. This however relies on the presupposition that our conversation was about coffee’s classification in a culinary context. The context later became a point of contention as the idea of ‘what is a soup’ and ‘what is technically a soup’ tends to differ quite significantly.
4
Matt
Agreed
But if you redefine to allow coffee to qualify then it's essentially the same
Matt’s counterpoint ends up being very close to a No True Scotsman fallacy. While he is not redefining the entire argument to suit his conclusion, he does redefine coffee as a vegetable.
A major counterpoint to my criticism is that this is not a redefinition at all in a different context. In a culinary context or the context of general understanding it would be ridiculous to call coffee a vegetable, but in a technical context coffee inarguably conforms to the definition of a vegetable. In fact ‘vegetable’ refers to any edible plant matter.

1
Matt
Wikipedia also lists juice as a possible liquid ingredient of soup, and coffee is bean juice
Matt uses a Fallacy of Division at this point.
His claim follows the form:
  1. Juice is a possible ingredient of soup
  2. Coffee is a type of juice
  3. Therefore coffee is soup
Of course this also relies on Wikipedia as a source for what possible ingredients of soup are, as well as the premise that coffee is a type of juice. The most heinous part of this point though is the idea that being a part of a greater whole, or an ingredient in soup, is equivalent to being that greater whole.
2

Here I propose an absurd example to emphasize the failure of Matt’s fallacy.
3

Matt justifies his argument by comparing juice to stock. Formalized:
  1. Stock can be soup
  2. Juice is similar to stock (implied)
  3. Therefore juice can be soup
While his premises are quite weak, this argument closely follows Hypothetical Syllogism:
  1. A →B
  2. B→C
  3. Therefore A→C

Matt asserts that he was trying to say something different. While I’m sure it was unintentional, it is still false. I’m not sure how do define what he did with this line. It’s similar to a red herring, but too related to truly count. Perhaps there is another term for this type of fallacy.

While I’ve focussed a great deal on the use of fallacies is our discourse I’d like to also include a more successful exchange. Boyle starts off by clarifying Matt’s definition of soup into a concise phrase.
2

Boyle also lets Matt confirm that he has understood correctly to avoid using a strawman argument against him.
3
Boyle
Like, boiling water is soup then
By considering Matt’s definition, Boyle has come to a contradiction. This is an effective use of the Reductio ad absurdum logical form to counter Matt’s point.
Formalized, the logic follows:
  1. Consider the definition “any liquid that has received heat is soup”
  2. As a liquid that has received heat, boiling water must then be a soup
  3. Boiling water is not a soup. This is a contradiction, thus the premise that “any liquid that has received heat is soup” must be false
1

While Danielle’s message it both interesting and new information, it also is an example of an Appeal to Questionable Authority and Equivocation. The amish are clearly no more qualified in defining soup than anyone else, and using them (or more accurately amish365.com) as proof for coffee’s classification is a questionable authority fallacy. The equivocation part of this argument comes in equating “coffee soup being soup” to “coffee being soup”.
1

At this point Matt asserts that a clear soup (like a soup without solids) is equivalent to broth, and therefore a broth is also a soup. While calling clear soup and broth the same thing is a straightforward claim to make, assuming that a clear soup (and therefore broth) is a type of soup because it has soup in the name is another example of equivocation.
